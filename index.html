Actual Facts (Hidden in the Shadows)

The dystopian reality: Sylvan Springs shut off comments on their government-owned Facebook page to prevent talk about the election, stifling free speech in the name of "order."

This action raises serious First Amendment concerns, as it may constitute impermissible viewpoint discrimination and content-based restrictions on public forums. Relevant case law includes:

  • Lindke v. Freed, 601 U.S. 187 (2024): The Supreme Court established a test for determining when a public official's social media activity constitutes state action, potentially making blocking or restricting access a First Amendment violation if done in an official capacity.
  • O’Connor-Ratcliff v. Garnier, 601 U.S. 205 (2024): Companion case to Lindke, emphasizing that officials cannot block critics from social media pages used for official purposes without violating free speech rights.
  • Knight First Amendment Institute at Columbia University v. Trump, 928 F.3d 226 (2d Cir. 2019): Held that blocking users from a government official's social media account based on their viewpoints violates the First Amendment, as the interactive space constitutes a public forum.
  • Davison v. Randall, 912 F.3d 666 (4th Cir. 2019): Banning a user from a government official's Facebook page for critical comments was ruled unconstitutional viewpoint discrimination.

While shutting off all comments might seem content-neutral, doing so specifically to suppress election-related discussions can be seen as a pretext for viewpoint discrimination, barred under these precedents. Governments cannot close public forums merely to avoid disfavored speech.
