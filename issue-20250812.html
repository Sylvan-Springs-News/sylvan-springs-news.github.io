OATH? WHAT OATH?: Mayor, Town Council Doubles-Down on Anti-1st Amendment Actions for Municipal Election Season

When did we get to a point where government could choose what you're allowed to speak about?

Sources: US Constitution, Town Council Meeting Recording

Original Story: August 13, 2025, based on August 12, 2025 Council Meeting

There have been two Sylvan Springs Town Council meetings since mayoral candidate, Aaron Wheeler (R) applied/announced that he was running for election. July 15, 2025, which was when we were told the Town Council voted to disable the open comment section on the Sylvan Springs, AL government-run Facebook page.

This meeting was a little different from past meetings. There was a sign-in sheet at front, which people can't be forced to sign, else it would violate the 4th Amendment. There was a meeting agenda printout (PDF link). There was a Jefferson County Sheriff Deputy, who was there representing JCSO regarding National Night Out. That seemed a little coordinated for it to be a coincidence, but it is what it is. We respect any man with a mustache as glorious and well kept as that deputy's.

In our interview with Stev Parsons, we asked him what the components of the 1st Amendment was, and he did have trouble answering. We helped him, and in hindsight as an interviewer, I was wrong. I should have reported it as it really was. As mayor, and members of the Town Council, more than likely they swore an oath to uphold and defend the US Constitution. However, in 2025, the first election in over 20 years for mayor of Sylvan Springs, it seems that our elected officials either aren't aware of what they swore an oath to, or they are aware and they choose to ignore/discard/trample on the most important, basic and supreme law of this great nation which made us great - The Constitution of the United States of America.

Early on, we raised our concerns about the 1st Amendment implications of this during our interview with Stev Parsons. Where we disagree, is we believe that the 1st Amendment extends to any open comment thread that had previously been open and run by government, whether it is in a town council meeting or on the town's official Facebook page. The Supreme Court also seems to agree with this notion.

Case Law
In the meeting, during public comment, it was shared that the town's action likely runs afoul of recent case law. To that, some council members either abruptly or angrily interrupted and fired back, that the town's attorneys reviewed it. We don't know about you, but last I recall, the Supreme Court of the United States opinions sort of outranks the lawyers for a 1500-population town. In trying to discuss with Jason Reed of the town council, that discussion went more like an argument, so we gave up on him. We aren't attorneys, so we can't just cite case law from memory like a Bible verse, and none of them can either. I think he knows that.

The short answer is: If a government-run Facebook page (or a government official acting with official authority) shuts off comments to stop discussion about the upcoming election, that's very likely unconstitutional viewpoint (or at least content) discrimination. If it were the page turning off all comments for everyone, for neutral reasons not related to what might be said, or if it might "embarrass" the municipality, courts have generally said that IS permissible.

In Davison v Randall (4th Cir. 2019), the court held the "interactive space" of an official Facebook page is a public forum; blocking a critic for accusing officials of corruption was viewpoint discrimination and violated the First Amendment. It is highly likely, that if the Town Council of Sylvan Springs turned off all comments for the purposes of prior restraint, preventing discussion about the election ahead of time that it would also fail the test and be ruled unconstitutional.

Public forum rules apply to government social media. Classic doctrine (Perry/Cornelius) allows governments to set reasonable viewpoint-neutral rules in limited or designated public forums, but viewpoint discrimination is never allowed. Rosenberger underscores that this type of discrimination is the "egregious" First Amendment violation even in limited forums. Shutting comments because they're about elections targets subject matter (and often viewpoint) and is presumptively unlawful.
