In history there have been things we've done with that revenue, such as the tornado shelter, as a result of our look to find those resoures to avoid having out of pocket money. Revenue is tight in small towns, based on the tax revenue sources. Foundational Beliefs

Q1 - Why are you running for mayor?

Stev has been committed for this long, and we continue to improve each year moving foward. I want that to continue. We have grandkids and great grandkids here. It's home, and we enjoy it. We want to keep that going. Our past mayors, councils have worked hard to ensure our town is a reputable town, they hear Sylvan Springs, recognize the name, and that it is a nice place to live.
Will we get a big box store? Who knows, but whatever resources are available, and whatever we can do to make our citizens complete and whole, that's what we're looking at.
Stev is happy with knowing in Sylvan Springs, that he can lay down at night and know they are safe.

Q2 - What are your beliefs about the U.S. Constitution and the Bill of Rights? Should citizens ever have to trade one Constitutional right for another?

Our forefathers wrote it and it is what it is. I wouldn't change it. It isn't meant to be changed. Citizens should not have to trade a right for another.

Q3 - Can you name the five freedoms guaranteed by the first amendment?

Interviewer Notes: Steve got 5/5. Please note: These are easy to miss/forget as an average citizen, and many officers who swore an oath to the Constitution can't answer this question on-the-spot. The answers are Religion, Speech, Press, Assembly, Petition.

For our readers, here's an accurate AI-generated definition, boiling it down:

  • Religion - This component protects the right to practice any religion or no religion at all. It prevents the government from establishing an official religion and guarantees the free exercise of religion.
  • Speech - This right ensures that individuals can express their thoughts and opinions without government interference. It allows for public debate, criticism of the government, and advocacy for change.
  • Press - This right safeguards the independence of the media, allowing journalists to report on government activities without fear of retaliation. It plays a crucial role in holding those in power accountable. The citizens are the press, corporation not required.
  • Assembly - This freedom allows individuals to gather in public spaces to express their views, protest injustices, and advocate for change. It ensures that citizens can come together to voice their concerns.
  • Petition - This right enables individuals to address grievances to the government and seek redress for issues that affect them. It empowers citizens to hold elected officials accountable and advocate for policies that align with their beliefs.

No.

Public records, like fire department call data, can highlight issues (e.g., illegal burning) or inform prevention efforts. Q5 - Should town-owned social media allow free speech like in-person forums?

Stev: "There is free speech. Can we prevent that from happening? No. Anyone that has ever come to town hall has been allowed to speak. Some choose to do a yelling match, and that was not allowed, until some of the attitude was changed. Done in the proper way, they can speak, and we'll listen."
Informed Stev, that the town FB page had comments disabled.
Stev's followup is that his opinion is, if there's something to say, best to say it face to face, especially if not agreed on. Stev says, "Today in social media, people can say anything, and they don't care. Once it hits social media, you can't bring it back. I've seen too many people hurt, harmed, affected badly, be it business or person. It's almost like we hide behind and throw snowballs, rather than having a conversation.""

Fiscal Policy

Q1 - Will you be supporting raising taxes to accomplish the goals you have as mayor?

Stev: "We will evaluate, but I don't know that we would actually do that unless it is the right thing to do."
"Sylvan Springs doesn't set the taxes, it would have to be a Jefferson County thing. That is not something I would like to do, but there are so many other options for funding. I would rather reach out to other areas to obtain funding in different ways, instead of adding tax."
"The town's budget is balanced, and we are debt free, with money in the bank."
"To build the town hall building, we did a bond, and we still have money in that account after building, so we are still in good shape financially. With what's out there for other project funding, there's opportunities. We prefer to operate without debt. Financially the town is very sound."

Local Issues

Q1 - Fire Department Governance: Do you support the fire department's current autonomy in its operations?

"At this point in time, if it isn't broken, no need to change. It's working very well so far."

Interviewer Note: The current governance model has the fire chief in charge of operational decision, with council oversight, inclusive of large spending decisions.

Q2 - Infrastructure/Roads: We heard pot holes on Sunset Dr was a concern. AL-269 seems to be a state road not getting a lot of care. What is your proposal to fix this ongoing issue?

We've been in the process, and have hired an independent contractor doing the patching work. We used to be able to go through the county, and have them come to do that patch work, but the county can no longer manage that. Part of it is the new hospital in McCalla. Jefferson County is doing the road work for the hospital, and that took them out of being able to help smaller municipalities.
The thing to keep in mind, paving roads is a huge financial cost, filling potholes, or paving a few miles. We try to put as much money as we can into the roads so that we'll have it. We also can't do these jobs in the winter time, only Spring/Summer. We've gotten several done since hiring a contractor.
Dropoff on Sunset Dr, the biggest problem with that road is when you go around the curve, it's a drop off. There's not a lot of room to put anything there. That's where we have difficulties, maintaining something that'll stay. There have been rods driven in to try to shore it up. There's a drainage ditch going through, and water going off the hill cutting into the hill.
This is under ongoing examination, a hot topic and a high priority.

Q2 - Some residents are concerned about crime, from individuals from outside our town. There was previous talk of rental property restrictions or fees in Sylvan Springs Council Meetings, noting that this could be a discrimination concern. How would new restrictions on rental properties reduce crime?

Not thinking that would reduce crime. First, we need to look at how many rentals we have. I don't think there's that many rentals. A fee might deter some of it. I don't know that you could mandate any restrictions. It may come down to private property rights.

Q3 - Will you impose new restrictions on landowners’ property use?

I'm open only if it warrants. Need to be careful when trying to regulate. We've got enough restrictions already in place, and most of the time it's a matter of communicating/knowledge first, before we start putting things in place.

Economic Development

Q1 - How will you attract businesses to Sylvan Springs?

Everyone thinks we want a fast food place, but there's limits on what they will do. Some time back, we did contact the Jack's company. We found them to be easier to deal with on locations, but their criteria would not fit because of one in Pleasant Grove. There's criteria there of course.
That being said, if I was looking for more business, I'd look for another category, not sure what that would be. Thinking about people who would open businesses like services businesses, quality local businesses.

Interviewer Note: To illustrate the considerations of running a business, we will use a fast-food restaurant (like Jack's) as an example.
Sylvan Springs population in 2023 was 1559, with 57% being over 18 (889) (Source: CensusReporter.org). A typical fast food restaurant requires an average of 200-300 customers daily in order to cover its costs (Source: BusinessDojo). This means at least 22%, or 1 in 4 adult Sylvan Springs residents would have to choose to eat fast food daily, for a fast-food business to survive. That doesn't sound healthy or sustainable. In closing, new businesses will need to have an understanding of their market area, and how to turn a profit with a good flow of business to survive.

Q2 - How will you address underperforming schools that Sylvan Springs is zoned for?

We are all about education, and increasing that opportunity. Home schools are out there, there's a lot of private schools still out there today. Jefferson County is not into changing zones. It would have to be on our end or find some kind of resources who have knowledge on that topic to get the information out there.
